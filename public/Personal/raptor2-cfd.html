Abstract

This report presents a comprehensive Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) study of the internal flow dynamics within the combustion chamber and nozzle of SpaceX's Raptor 2 engine, a cutting-edge Full-Flow Staged Combustion (FFSC) engine fueled by methane (CH‚ÇÑ) and oxygen (O‚ÇÇ). The objective of this research is to characterize the complex flow behavior, combustion efficiency, and performance parameters under both sea-level and vacuum conditions, providing critical insights into next-generation propulsion system optimization.

Exhaust gases accelerated to 3,420 m/s with a Mach number of 3.93, validating the model's fidelity. Context

Introduction & Motivation

Rocket propulsion plays a critical role in space exploration, enabling vehicles to overcome Earth's gravity and travel into orbit and beyond. At the heart of these propulsion systems lies the combustion chamber and nozzle, where high-pressure, high-temperature gases are generated and accelerated to produce thrust. Understanding the complex fluid dynamics within these components is essential for optimizing performance and ensuring structural integrity.

Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has become an indispensable tool in modern propulsion system design, allowing engineers to simulate and analyze internal flows with high precision, significantly reducing the need for costly and time-consuming physical testing. In this context, SpaceX's Raptor 2 engine stands as a state-of-the-art example of a full-flow staged combustion engine powered by liquid methane and liquid oxygen (methalox), offering high performance and reusability.

SpaceX Raptor 2 engine showing internal components and flow paths

Overview of the Raptor 2 Engine

The Raptor 2 engine, developed by SpaceX, represents a major leap in rocket propulsion technology. It is a full-flow staged combustion engine (FFSC) that uses methane (CH‚ÇÑ) and liquid oxygen (LOX) as propellants, making it suitable for long-term missions, including potential Mars colonization. Designed for high efficiency and reusability, Raptor 2 delivers significantly more thrust and is mechanically simpler compared to its predecessor.

Full-Flow Staged Combustion (FFSC)

Full-flow staged combustion (FFSC) is a type of staged combustion cycle with a dual-shaft design that uses both oxidizer-rich and fuel-rich preburners, where the entire supply of both propellants passes through the turbines. The fuel turbopump is driven by the fuel-rich preburner, and the oxidizer turbopump is driven by the oxidizer-rich preburner.

Historical Context

As of 2024, only four FFSC rocket engines have been tested on the ground: the Soviet RD-270 (1960s), the U.S. Integrated Powerhead Demonstrator (mid-2000s), SpaceX's Raptor (first tested February 2019), and Stoke Space's Nova engine (2024). The first flight test of an FFSC engine occurred on July 25, 2019, when SpaceX flew their Raptor engine on the Starhopper test vehicle. As of January 2025, the Raptor remains the only FFSC engine to have flown on a launch vehicle.

Approach

Methodology

The methodology combines multiple modeling tools in a structured workflow: preliminary thermodynamic analysis via NASA's Chemical Equilibrium with Applications (CEA), geometric refinement using Rocket Propulsion Analysis (RPA), and preburner combustion modeling with Cantera in Python. A 2D axisymmetric CFD model was developed, integrating high-resolution meshing, realistic boundary conditions, and turbulence-chemistry interaction based on the GRI-Mech 3.0 chemical mechanism.

Phase 1: Preliminary Design Using NASA CEA

Objective: Obtain key thermodynamic and performance parameters

Used NASA CEA to calculate specific impulse, characteristic velocity, combustion temperature, and exhaust gas properties. These parameters formed the foundation for defining initial geometry including throat area, expansion ratio, chamber length, and diameters.

Phase 2: Geometry Refinement with RPA

Tool: Rocket Propulsion Analysis software

Refined preliminary design using RPA to optimize nozzle geometry, validate thermochemical results from NASA CEA, and generate detailed performance predictions under realistic conditions. Adjusted expansion area ratio to 36 to match Raptor 2's 1.3m exit diameter.

Phase 3: Preburner Combustion Modeling

Tool: Cantera (Python)

Developed custom Python program to simulate preburner equilibrium compositions for both fuel-rich and oxidizer-rich preburners. Determined outlet temperatures (808K for fuel, 707K for oxidizer) and species mass fractions using GRI-Mech 3.0 mechanism.

Phase 4: 2D Axisymmetric Geometry Creation

Tool: SolidWorks

Created 2D axisymmetric model based on RPA output to reduce computational cost. Designed simplified injectors with coaxial geometry sized using discharge coefficient methods and pressure drop calculations (24% for oxidizer, 9.5% for fuel).

Phase 5: Mesh Generation

Technique: Structured quadrilateral meshing

Generated high-quality mesh using face meshing with quadrilateral elements. Applied 20 divisions near walls to capture viscous effects. Used size division method for controlled mesh density: 130 divisions at throat, 100 at outlet, 260 along axis/wall.

Phase 6: CFD Simulation Setup

Tool: ANSYS Fluent

Configured steady-state simulation using SIMPLE algorithm for pressure-velocity coupling. Implemented k-œâ SST turbulence model with GRI-Mech 3.0 chemistry (53 species, 325 reactions). Applied presumed beta-PDF combustion model with mixture fraction approach.

Phase 7: Multiple Simulation Cases

Strategy: Parametric study approach

Conducted 8 simulations varying O/F ratio (3.0 vs 3.6), inlet conditions (liquid vs gaseous, pure vs realistic fractions), and ambient pressure (vacuum vs sea level) to understand FFSC benefits and design trade-offs.

Phase 8: Post-Processing and Validation

Analysis: Performance comparison and validation

Analyzed pressure, temperature, velocity, Mach number, and species distributions. Corrected numerical artifacts in Mach field near walls. Compared results with actual Raptor 2 performance data, achieving <3% deviation in key parameters.

Governing Equations

The mathematical model employed in this study is based on the steady-state conservation equations of mass, momentum, energy, and species. Given the highly turbulent and reactive environment, Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations with Favre-averaging were used to account for compressibility effects.

Key Modeling Assumptions

  • Steady-state flow conditions
  • 2D axisymmetric geometry
  • Adiabatic walls (no heat transfer)
  • Non-reactive wall surfaces
  • k-œâ SST turbulence closure
  • Presumed beta-PDF combustion model
  • GRI-Mech 3.0 chemical kinetics
  • Unity Lewis number assumption

Injector Design

The sizing of the injectors is a critical aspect of the design. We used 640 coaxial injectors distributed across the injector plate. The injectors were sized using discharge coefficient methods (Cd = 0.6) with calculated pressure drops of 72.2 bar for oxidizer and 28.8 bar for fuel, corresponding to 24% and 9.5% of chamber pressure respectively.

Findings

Simulation Results

Convergence Analysis

The simulation was executed for 5,000 iterations, after which convergence was reached. Although the residuals of continuity did not fall below the typical threshold of 1√ó10‚Åª¬≥, the residuals of other variables showed stable convergence. Key physical quantities including mass flow rate, outlet pressure, temperature, velocity, and Mach number all stabilized, confirming solution reliability.

Convergence Criteria Met

  • Mass flow rate reached target: -718 kg/s (outward flow)
  • Outlet temperature stabilized with minor acceptable oscillations
  • Velocity and Mach number converged to steady values
  • Pressure distribution showed physical consistency
  • All species mass fractions reached equilibrium

Pressure Distribution

The simulation recorded a chamber pressure of approximately 305 bar compared to the expected 300 bar from Raptor 2 specifications, representing only a 1.7% difference. This excellent agreement validates the modeling approach. The pressure remains relatively stable within the chamber before dropping significantly through the convergent-divergent nozzle as thermal energy converts to kinetic energy.

Static pressure distribution from combustion chamber through nozzle exit

Temperature Distribution

The temperature within the combustion chamber reaches a peak of approximately 3,720 K, confirming efficient combustion of the methane-oxygen mixture. After combustion, hot gases expand through the nozzle with significant temperature drop as internal energy converts to kinetic energy. The adiabatic wall assumption naturally leads to higher gas temperatures than real engines with regenerative cooling.

Temperature field showing combustion zone and nozzle expansion cooling

Velocity and Mach Number

Gas velocity at the nozzle exit reaches approximately 3,530 m/s, indicating highly efficient acceleration consistent with methane-oxygen propulsion systems. The velocity profile shows a sharp drop to 0 m/s near walls due to the no-slip boundary condition, creating a boundary layer where viscous effects are significant.

The Mach number distribution validates the nozzle design: Mach 1 is achieved at the throat (transition from subsonic to supersonic flow), and approximately Mach 3.94 at the exit. A numerical artifact showing unphysically high Mach numbers near walls was corrected by redefining Mach number as the ratio of local velocity to local speed of sound.

Discussion

Validation Against RPA and Raptor 2

The comparison between ANSYS CFD simulation results, RPA predictions, and actual Raptor 2 engine data reveals excellent agreement across all major performance metrics. Chamber pressure shows only 1.33% deviation from the nominal value, thrust differs by 2.17%, and specific impulse by 2.14% when compared to real engine data.

Why Our Results Are Slightly Higher

While our simulation shows marginally better performance than the real Raptor 2, this does not suggest our design is superior. The differences arise from idealized assumptions:

  • No regenerative cooling: Real engines divert propellant through cooling channels, reducing combustion mass flow
  • Adiabatic walls: No heat losses to engine structure
  • Perfect mixing: Idealized injector uniformity
  • No losses: Turbopump efficiency, boundary layer, and friction losses not included

SpaceX optimizes for reliability, durability, manufacturability, and safety margins rather than absolute theoretical efficiency.

Why Full-Flow Staged Combustion?

The performance comparison between simulation cases clearly demonstrates why SpaceX employs the FFSC cycle rather than simpler alternatives. Cases with cryogenic liquid propellants (simulations 2 and 6) showed significantly lower performance due to cold inlet temperatures and incomplete vaporization. Pure gaseous propellants at high temperature (simulations 3 and 7) showed improved performance but are physically unrealistic without preburning.

FFSC Advantages

  • Complete propellant gasification before main chamber
  • Elevated inlet temperatures enable efficient combustion
  • Better turbine efficiency with lower temperature drops
  • Improved cooling via regenerative propellant routing
  • Higher chamber pressure capability
  • Enhanced thermal management

Comparison to Other Cycles

  • vs Gas Generator: No wasted propellant, higher Isp
  • vs Single-Shaft SC: No inter-propellant seal, more uniform combustion
  • Reusability: Up to 1000 flights targeted for Raptor
  • Reliability: Lower turbine temperatures extend lifetime

Why O/F = 3.6 Instead of 3.0?

Simulations clearly show that O/F = 3.0 provides superior performance: 6.63% higher vacuum thrust (2.57 MN vs 2.41 MN) and 6.11% higher specific impulse (364.9 s vs 342.6 s). However, SpaceX chose O/F = 3.6 for critical system-level reasons related to propellant storage and vehicle design.

System-Level Trade-offs

Storage Considerations: A lower O/F ratio requires more methane storage. Since liquid methane has relatively low density (~422 kg/m¬≥) compared to liquid oxygen (~1,141 kg/m¬≥), this necessitates significantly larger and heavier methane tanks. Larger tanks require:

  • Increased structural mass for tank walls and supports
  • More insulation to maintain cryogenic temperatures
  • Complex pressurization and management systems
  • Greater internal volume reducing payload capacity
  • Challenges in vehicle layout and center of mass

Optimal Balance: O/F = 3.6 represents a well-balanced trade-off between engine performance, vehicle mass distribution, structural efficiency, reusability, and overall mission feasibility. The slight performance penalty is outweighed by dramatic improvements in vehicle design practicality.

Vacuum vs Sea Level Performance

The comparison between vacuum and sea-level conditions reveals fundamental rocket physics principles. Vacuum operation delivers 6.51% higher thrust (2.52 MN vs 2.35 MN) and correspondingly higher specific impulse due to more complete nozzle expansion without atmospheric back pressure.

Vacuum Conditions

  • Thrust: 2.52 MN
  • Isp: 357.6 s
  • Exit velocity: 3,460 m/s
  • Exit pressure: 25,100 Pa
  • No external resistance to flow expansion
  • Gases expand more fully

Sea Level Conditions

  • Thrust: 2.35 MN
  • Isp: 334.3 s
  • Exit velocity: 3,420 m/s
  • Exit pressure: 25,400 Pa
  • Atmospheric pressure resists expansion
  • Slightly higher back pressure

These differences underscore why vacuum-specific performance metrics are always higher and why nozzle design involves trade-offs to optimize expansion across both sea-level and vacuum phases of flight. The Raptor 2's area ratio of 34.34 represents a compromise optimized for the full flight trajectory.

Engineering Contributions

Methodological Contributions

Established a robust workflow combining NASA CEA, RPA, Cantera, and ANSYS Fluent for high-fidelity rocket engine simulation. Demonstrated the importance of realistic preburner modeling for accurate FFSC engine predictions. Validated the use of GRI-Mech 3.0 with presumed PDF combustion for methalox propulsion systems.

Physical Insights

Quantified the performance benefits of FFSC over simpler cycles. Explained system-level trade-offs in O/F ratio selection. Demonstrated critical importance of preburner outlet conditions on main chamber performance. Identified areas where simplified assumptions (adiabatic walls) impact results.

Limitations & Assumptions

Key Simplifications

  • Adiabatic walls: No heat transfer to engine structure; real engines use regenerative cooling
  • Steady-state: Transient effects during ignition, startup, and shutdown not captured
  • 2D axisymmetric: Three-dimensional effects and asymmetries neglected
  • Simplified injectors: Detailed spray dynamics and atomization not fully resolved
  • Perfect mixing: Injector-to-injector variations not modeled
  • No structural coupling: Wall deformation and thermal expansion not included

Future Work Recommendations

Regenerative Cooling Integration

Model coolant flow through chamber walls with conjugate heat transfer to evaluate thermal loading and chamber wall temperatures. This will provide more realistic performance predictions accounting for coolant extraction from main flow.

Transient Analysis

Conduct time-dependent simulations to capture ignition dynamics, startup/shutdown sequences, and combustion instability phenomena. Critical for understanding engine control and safety margins.

3D Modeling

Implement full three-dimensional geometry to capture radial flow features, injector pattern effects, and potential asymmetries in the combustion chamber and nozzle.

Advanced Turbulence

Employ Large Eddy Simulation (LES) or hybrid RANS-LES approaches to improve resolution of turbulent structures and better predict combustion-turbulence interactions.

Detailed Spray Modeling

Implement Lagrangian particle tracking or Eulerian-Eulerian multiphase models to capture realistic injector spray, droplet breakup, and vaporization dynamics.

Combustion Instability

Investigate acoustic modes and potential combustion instabilities through unsteady simulation and frequency domain analysis to ensure stable operation.

Experimental Validation

Compare predictions with subscale test data when available to further validate modeling assumptions and refine simulation parameters for improved accuracy.
