Context

Introduction & Motivation

Fluid-structure interaction (FSI) represents a fundamental research domain in aeronautical engineering, particularly crucial for studying wing behavior under aerodynamic loads. When fluid flows around a flexible structure like an aircraft wing, the generated pressures induce deformations which, in turn, modify the fluid flow. This bidirectional interaction can have significant consequences on aircraft performance, stability, and safety.

In the context of fixed-wing drone design, understanding FSI phenomena becomes crucial for optimizing structure while ensuring mechanical resistance. Drones, due to their lightweight nature and operational versatility, are particularly sensitive to aerodynamic loads during surveillance, transport, or observation missions. Precise modeling of structural response under aerodynamic loading enables not only design validation but also identification of critical zones requiring reinforcement or optimization.

Project Scope

This work presents a complete numerical study of drone wing behavior subjected to aerodynamic loads. The adopted approach relies on a one-way coupling methodology using ANSYS Workbench platform. First, a CFD (Computational Fluid Dynamics) simulation is performed in ANSYS Fluent to determine pressure distribution around the wing at incidence. Results from this simulation are then transferred to ANSYS Mechanical to perform static structural analysis, thus evaluating induced deformations and stresses.

Why Fluid-Structure Interaction?

FSI analysis is essential in aerospace engineering for several critical reasons:

  • Performance Optimization: Understanding how aerodynamic forces deform the wing allows engineers to predict actual flight characteristics
  • Safety Assessment: Identifying stress concentrations helps prevent structural failures during flight
  • Weight Reduction: Accurate load prediction enables optimal material distribution, reducing unnecessary weight
  • Flutter Prevention: FSI analysis helps predict and avoid dangerous aeroelastic phenomena
  • Design Validation: Virtual testing reduces the need for expensive physical prototypes

Approach

Methodology

The study employs a systematic two-phase approach: computational fluid dynamics analysis followed by structural analysis. This one-way coupling methodology provides an efficient first approximation of FSI effects while maintaining reasonable computational costs.

Phase 1: Geometry Preparation

CAD Modeling: Wing geometry created in CAD software and imported into ANSYS

Fluid domain created using Enclosure tool with sufficient margins upstream, downstream, and laterally to avoid boundary influence on flow. Domain dimensions: 300mm upstream, 250mm downstream, 250mm lateral, 200mm vertical.

Phase 2: CFD Mesh Generation

Strategy: Fine mesh near wing surface, coarser mesh in far-field

Used Patch Conforming method with tetrahedral elements. Body sizing for wing: 5√ó10‚Åª¬≥ m, Face sizing for inlet/outlet: 1√ó10‚Åª¬≤ m. Quadratic elements for improved accuracy. Total mesh: refined around wing to capture pressure gradients.

Phase 3: CFD Simulation Setup

Solver: ANSYS Fluent with density-based solver

Steady-state RANS equations with k-œâ SST turbulence model. Inlet velocity: 70 m/s at 15¬∞ angle of attack. Outlet: pressure outlet at 0 Pa gauge. Wing surface: no-slip wall boundary condition.

Phase 4: CFD Solution & Convergence

Iterations: 1000 iterations to achieve convergence

Monitored residuals (continuity, momentum, turbulence) all below 10‚Åª¬≥. Additional monitoring of outlet pressure, velocity, mass flow rate, and aerodynamic coefficients to ensure stable solution. Computation time: approximately 2.5 hours.

Phase 5: Pressure Data Transfer

One-Way Coupling: CFD results exported to Mechanical

Pressure distribution from converged CFD solution transferred to ANSYS Mechanical as "Imported Pressure". This enables structural analysis with realistic aerodynamic loading without iterative coupling.

Automatic meshing with Body Sizing control (5√ó10‚Åª¬≥ m). Quadratic tetrahedral elements for accurate stress and deformation predictions. Refined mesh in areas of expected stress concentration.

Computed total deformation field and von Mises equivalent stress distribution. Identified maximum stress locations and deformation patterns. Evaluated safety factors against material yield strength.

Structural Response Analysis

The structural analysis reveals a highly conservative design with stress levels far below material limits. While this ensures structural integrity, it also indicates significant potential for weight optimization.

Positive Aspects

  • Excellent structural safety margin
  • Minimal deformation under design loads
  • No risk of yielding or plastic deformation
  • Stress distribution follows expected patterns
  • CFD-to-FEM coupling workflow validated

Areas for Improvement

  • Solid wing geometry unrealistic for aviation
  • Excessive safety factor indicates over-design
  • Mass could be significantly reduced
  • One-way coupling doesn't capture FSI feedback
  • Static analysis misses dynamic effects

Limitations of Current Model

The current study, while technically sound, has several important limitations that should be acknowledged:

Modeling Assumptions

  • Solid Wing Geometry: Real aircraft wings use lightweight structures with ribs, spars, and thin skin - not solid material
  • One-Way Coupling: Aerodynamic loads affect structure, but structural deformation doesn't feed back to fluid simulation
  • Static Analysis: Dynamic effects, vibrations, and transient phenomena not captured
  • Material Homogeneity: Assumes uniform material properties throughout wing
  • Linear Elasticity: No consideration of material nonlinearities or large deformations

Real-World Wing Structures

Actual aircraft and drone wings employ sophisticated lightweight structures that achieve optimal strength-to-weight ratios. The extremely low stress levels (1.16 MPa vs 280 MPa limit) obtained in this study clearly demonstrate that a solid wing structure is impractical.

Typical Wing Construction

  • Skin: Thin outer shell (0.5-2mm) carrying aerodynamic loads
  • Ribs: Maintain airfoil shape and transfer loads to spars
  • Spars: Main longitudinal members resisting bending
  • Stringers: Longitudinal stiffeners preventing buckling
  • Result: 70-90% weight reduction vs solid structure

Benefits of Proper Design

  • Dramatic mass reduction without sacrificing strength
  • Improved fuel efficiency and flight endurance
  • Better payload capacity
  • Optimized material utilization
  • Lower manufacturing costs (less material)

One-Way vs Two-Way Coupling

This study employed one-way coupling where fluid forces affect structure but structural deformation doesn't modify the flow. While computationally efficient, this approach has limitations:

Aspect One-Way Coupling (Used) Two-Way Coupling
Computational Cost Low - single CFD + single FEM High - multiple iterations required
Accuracy Good for small deformations Excellent for all deformation levels
Phenomena Captured Static load effects only Dynamic coupling, flutter, divergence
Use Cases Preliminary design, rigid structures Final design, flexible structures
Convergence Always converges May require stabilization techniques

When One-Way Coupling is Sufficient

Our approach is justified because:

  • Maximum deformation (0.1 mm) is negligible compared to chord length (~200 mm) - only 0.05%
  • Such small deformations have minimal effect on aerodynamic flow field
  • Stress levels well within elastic range - no material nonlinearities
  • Provides efficient preliminary assessment for design validation

However, two-way coupling would be necessary for:

  • Large deflections (>1% of characteristic dimension)
  • Flutter analysis and dynamic aeroelastic phenomena
  • Highly flexible structures (composite wings, membrane structures)
  • Final design validation before manufacturing

Practical Implications

The safety factor of 241 indicates the current design could support loads 241 times higher before reaching yield strength. This suggests several practical improvements:

  • Structural Optimization: Replace solid wing with lightweight structure (ribs + spars + skin) to reduce mass by 70-90%
  • Material Selection: Could potentially use lighter materials (composites, foam core) while maintaining adequate strength
  • Topology Optimization: Use computational methods to identify optimal material distribution
  • Weight Reduction Benefits: Improved endurance, higher payload capacity, better fuel efficiency
  • Cost Reduction: Less material usage reduces manufacturing costs

  • No Manufacturing Constraints: Design doesn't consider fabrication limitations or assembly

Recommendations for Future Work

Realistic Wing Structure Design

Replace solid geometry with lightweight structure incorporating ribs, spars, stringers, and thin skin. This is the most important improvement for practical application.

Two-Way FSI Coupling

Implement bidirectional coupling where structural deformation updates fluid domain geometry. Essential for flexible wing analysis and flutter prediction.

Dynamic Analysis

Perform transient structural analysis to capture vibration modes, frequency response, and time-dependent loading effects.

Parametric Study

Investigate effect of angle of attack (0-20¬∞), velocity (30-100 m/s), and material properties on structural response.

Optimization Studies

Apply topology optimization and shape optimization to minimize mass while maintaining adequate safety factors (2-3 instead of 241).

Composite Materials Analysis

Explore fiber-reinforced composites with anisotropic properties for further weight reduction and tailored stiffness.

Flutter & Divergence Analysis

Perform aeroelastic stability analysis to determine critical flutter speed and divergence boundaries.

Experimental Validation

Wind tunnel testing and strain gauge measurements on physical prototype to validate CFD and FEM predictions.
